History of Uranium

Discovered in 1789 by Martin Heinrich Klaproth, a German scientist.

Uranium was discovered by scientist Martin Heinrich Klaproth in the mineral pitchblende. Klaproth extracted a substance he belived to be pure uranium, however it was actually uranium dioxide (UO2). French chemist, Eugène-Melchoir Péligot, was the first to isolate Uranium by itself.

He did this by heating uranium dioxide with pottasium in a platinum crucible. Later, in 1896, French physicist Antoine Henri Becquerel was the first to discover radioactivity, by detecting it from a sample of uranium (Thomas Jefferson National Accelerator Facility - Office of Science Education, n.d.).

Modern Uses for Uranium

Power Plants, Bullets, Explosive, and Nuclear Tamper

Uranium is commonly to power powerplants through nuclear fission. When struck by a neutron, the nucleus releases energy by splitting into smaller fragments (some can be neutrons). Uranium-235 is capable of producing enough free neutrons to sustain a nuclear chain reaction. Generally depleted uranium (uranium when all of the radioactive isotopes are removed) is used for all other applications (Air University, n.d.). Depleted Uranium is highly dense and therefore is useful in bullets as its density allows it to pierece materials easily. It also explodes upon impact and burns at high degrees allowing it to be a useful explosive.

Societal Problems Solved

The Energy Problem (Environmental) and the Weaponry Problem (Political)

The biggest problem looming over human civilization right now is the energy crisis. Uranium is a semi-solution to it because it removes the need for fossil fuels. This reduces global warming, air pollution, and many other energy problems. Additionally Uranium is useful in many military applications. Depleted Uranium also can act as a nuclear tamper, which increases the yield (amount of energy released) of a nuclear bomb (Maeda, Saruta, & Inamori, n.d.). This results in more destructive weaponry. President Obama's controversial Iran Nuclear Deal had much to do with the fact that Iran had a lot of uranium — something many governments did not want Iran to have. Uranium is such a controverisal element because its usage has been linked to cancer, especially lukemia (Air University, n.d.).
